Chapter One

Designed for Life

Timeless design is not a style. It is a decision — to create forms that transcend trends and serve their owners for decades.

Walk into a home furnishing showroom in 2010 and you would have seen the trends of that year. Return in 2020 and almost nothing survived. The chairs, the tables, the colour palettes — gone, replaced by whatever that season demanded.

Ethnicraft's design philosophy is a direct rejection of this cycle. Every design decision is tested against one question: will this still feel right in twenty years? Not ten. Twenty.

This is harder than designing for today. Designing for today means you follow what's selling. Designing for twenty years means you have to understand something deeper — the proportions, materials, and forms that human beings consistently find beautiful across generations and cultures.

"Good design forms the foundation of a lifetime. Pieces meant to evolve with their owners and move across generations."

The Ethnicraft archive proves this works. Designs from the 2000s still sell today. Not because they are being kept artificially alive, but because customers see them in showrooms and respond to them without knowing how old they are. That is the test of timeless design.

The Eye dining chair — a form that has remained relevant across years of trend cycles.

Ethnicraft designs across multiple years — each as relevant today as when they were introduced.

What timeless design means in practice

∅

No trend dependency

Designs are not based on what is popular this year. They are based on proportional systems and material logic that have endured across centuries.

⬡

Adaptable aesthetics

The same table reads equally well in a Scandinavian apartment, a Belgian farmhouse, or a New York loft. That range is not luck. It is considered design.

◎

Restrained detail

Details that feel fresh rarely age well. Details that feel considered — proportional, structural, material — remain relevant. Ethnicraft designers work in this restrained space.

The Repair Kit

The most powerful thing you can tell a customer about solid wood furniture is this: when it gets damaged, you fix it. You do not replace it.

This is the argument that closes the price objection permanently. Solid wood furniture can be sanded, oiled, repaired, and refinished — indefinitely. A scratch on a solid oak dining table is not a tragedy. It is a 20-minute job.

Learn the care story well. Not because customers will all want to care for their furniture the moment they buy it — but because knowing you can repair it changes how they feel about the investment.

Light scratches on oiled oak

Fine scratches on oiled oak are among the easiest repairs in furniture care. The process: sand lightly with 220-grit sandpaper in the direction of the wood grain (never across — this leaves visible marks). Sand only the scratched area and a small margin around it. Wipe clean with a dry cloth, let the wood breathe for 10 minutes, then apply a thin coat of Ethnicraft natural wood oil with a clean cloth. Work it into the grain. Let it absorb for 15 minutes, then wipe off the excess. Done. The scratch is gone.

Retailer script

"If your child draws on it with a fork, no problem. Sand it gently, re-oil, and it is perfect again. You cannot do that with any other furniture material."

Water rings or heat marks

Water rings form when moisture is trapped in the surface of oiled wood. Heat marks (from hot cups or plates) cause similar white hazing. Neither is permanent. For fresh water rings: place a clean cotton cloth over the ring and apply a warm iron for 10–20 seconds. The heat draws the moisture out. Lift the cloth and the ring is often gone. If it persists, lightly sand with 220-grit and re-oil. For heat marks, the same process applies — but if the mark is dark (indicating actual scorching), sand more firmly before re-oiling.

Retailer script

"Even if your guests leave water rings on the table after dinner, you can usually remove them in five minutes with an iron. That is freedom. That is solid wood."

Deep scratches or dents

Deeper damage requires more patience but is still fully repairable. Start with coarser sandpaper (120 or 150 grit) to level the damaged area with the surrounding surface. Sand with the grain in long, even strokes. Progress through finer grits (180, then 220) to restore the surface smoothness. For dents in solid wood: place a damp cloth over the dent and apply a warm iron. The steam causes the wood fibres to swell back towards their original position. Finish with 220-grit sanding and re-oil. Important: this works with solid wood because you are working with the actual material. It cannot be done with veneer or MDF.

Retailer script

"A deep dent in a veneer or MDF table means replacement. A deep dent in solid oak means 30 minutes with sandpaper and oil. Which would you prefer?"

Routine oiling maintenance

Oiled solid wood furniture should be re-oiled once to twice per year — more frequently in dry climates or centrally heated homes. Signs that the wood needs oil: it feels dry, has a dusty appearance, or water no longer beads on the surface. Process: clean the surface thoroughly, let it dry completely, then apply Ethnicraft natural oil evenly with a cloth. Work it in circular motions, then with the grain. Allow 15–30 minutes absorption time. Wipe off all excess — oil left on the surface will become sticky. Allow 24 hours before heavy use. Do not use cooking oils or olive oil — these go rancid and discolour the wood.

Retailer script

"Think of oiling your table like watering a plant. Twice a year, 20 minutes each time. That is the total maintenance commitment for furniture that will outlive you."

Outdoor teak care

Teak is the great exception in wood care because it genuinely requires very little maintenance. Its natural oils protect it from moisture, insects, and UV damage without any intervention. Left outdoors untreated, teak will gradually turn silver-grey — this is not damage, it is oxidation of the natural oils on the surface. The underlying wood remains perfectly healthy and strong. If customers prefer to maintain the golden-honey colour of fresh teak, they should apply teak oil once per year after cleaning with a stiff brush and mild soapy water. But even if they never do anything — the furniture will outlast them.

Retailer script

"Teak is the only furniture material where the correct answer to 'how do I care for it?' is sometimes 'nothing at all.' It does the work itself."

What NOT to do

Never use harsh cleaning products — bleach, ammonia-based cleaners, or multi-surface sprays. These strip the protective oil and damage the wood fibres. Never soak with water — wipe up spills immediately. Extended water contact causes staining and can lead to swelling. Never use silicone-based polishes — they build up a film that prevents future oiling and is difficult to remove. Never place directly on radiators or in strong direct sunlight for extended periods — extreme heat and UV cause cracking and fading. Never sand across the grain — always with it. Cross-grain sanding leaves visible scratches that are difficult to remove.

Retailer script

"The list of things that will harm solid oak is short and mostly common sense. The list of things that will harm MDF is much longer — and most of them you can't fix."

Chapter Three

A New Life

Ethnicraft has built two programmes that extend the life of its furniture beyond the first owner. This is what real circular economy looks like.

The circular economy is not a marketing term for Ethnicraft. It is the logical extension of building furniture that is genuinely designed to last. If a piece can last 50 years, why should it only serve one owner?

Initiative 01

Live Light

Ethnicraft's rental programme allows customers to live with solid wood furniture without the permanence of ownership. Pieces are rented for a defined period, maintained by Ethnicraft, and returned — then restored and placed with new customers. For customers in transition (moving cities, temporary living situations) or those who simply want the freedom to change, Live Light provides access to quality without commitment.

Rental programme 01

Initiative 02

Re-loved

Re-loved is Ethnicraft's refurbishment and resale programme. Pieces returned from customers — through Live Light, trade-ins, or buy-backs — are assessed, restored to full quality (sanding, re-oiling, structural repair if needed), and resold at a new price point. The result: great furniture gets a second life, new customers access Ethnicraft quality at a lower entry price, and the environmental benefit of that original craftsmanship is extended for decades more.

Refurbishment & resale 02

Why this matters for retailers: These programmes are not just brand stories — they are commercial tools. They change the end-of-life conversation entirely.

When a customer says "but I don't want to be stuck with this table if I redecorate" — you have an answer. When they worry about the resale value — you have an answer. When they ask about what happens to the piece after they no longer want it — you have an answer.

This is the full circle of the sustainability argument: a material (solid wood) that lasts indefinitely, cared for by the customer, and backed by programmes that ensure the furniture remains in use long after the original purchase. That is a story no fast furniture brand can tell.

Quality furniture built for a second and third life — the promise behind Re-loved.

Chapter Four

Sell It Better

Retail coaching. The questions customers ask, the objections they raise — and the answers that convert.

The knowledge from all three Academy modules distils into one place: the moment a customer is standing in front of a piece and asking "why should I buy this?" Here are the conversations that will happen on your floor, and the answers that win them.

Retailer Coach

Every objection. Every answer. Ready to use.

Price objection

"This is €3,000 for a dining table. I can find the same size at IKEA for €400."

Your answer

"You are right that they look similar in a photograph. But let me ask you something: where does that €400 table end up in ten years? And where does this one end up? Solid oak at €3,000 over 30 years costs less than €100 per year. The €400 table replaced three times in the same period costs €120 per year — and three trips to the tip. Which is the economical choice?"

Maintenance concern

"I have kids. I'm worried about marks and scratches."

Your answer

"That is exactly why solid wood is the right choice for families. With MDF or veneer, a deep scratch means replacing a piece. With solid oak, it means 20 minutes with some sandpaper and oil — and the table looks perfect again. Every scratch tells a story, and you can erase any story you want to. I can show you how easy it is."

Sustainability concern

"Is cutting down trees really sustainable? I feel better buying recycled plastic furniture."

Your answer

"Ethicrafts wood is 100% traceable under the EU Deforestation Regulation — certified from sustainably managed forests where new trees are planted for every one harvested. And unlike recycled plastic, solid wood stores carbon, is biodegradable, can be repaired indefinitely, and does not off-gas microplastics into your home. Solid wood is the original sustainable material."

Aesthetic uncertainty

"I'm not sure this style will still work when I redecorate in a few years."

Your answer

"This design has been in the Ethnicraft range for over 10 years and is still one of our bestsellers — not because we're keeping it alive, but because customers walk in and choose it without knowing its age. That is the definition of timeless design. And if you ever want to change the look without changing the table — change the chairs around it, change the accessories on it. The table will adapt."

Origin question

"Where does the wood come from? I want to know it's not from deforestation."

Your answer

"Great question — I love that you're asking this. Ethnicraft is fully EUDR compliant, which means every piece of wood is geo-referenced back to its certified sustainable forest of origin. We know which forest, which country, which certification programme — and it is all legally verified. This table's oak came from responsibly managed European forests. I can get you the full documentation if you'd like it."

Knots & variation

"There are knots in the wood. Is this a defect? My neighbour's table doesn't have that."

Your answer

"Knots are part of the tree's growth story — they are where branches once grew. In solid wood, they are a mark of authenticity, not a defect. No two Ethnicraft pieces are identical, and that is exactly the point. You are not buying a mass-produced product. You are buying a piece of solid wood with its own individual character. That is what your neighbour's table, if it is MDF with a wood-print foil, can never have."
