Chapter One

The Indonesian Atelier

In 2018, Ethnicraft established a dedicated woodworking training facility in Indonesia. It was not a production line. It was an act of cultural preservation.

Indonesia has a centuries-old tradition of masterful woodworking. In Jepara — often called the "city of carving" — skills passed from master to apprentice for generations are at risk of disappearing as mass production replaces handwork.

Ethnicraft's training atelier was created to reverse this. The facility brings together experienced master craftspeople and younger artisans, creating a structured programme of skill transfer: hand-carving, traditional joinery, surface finishing, and quality reading of raw timber — knowledge that cannot be found in a manual.

This is not corporate social responsibility window dressing. This is vertically integrated production with a human heart. Every complex organic shape in the Ethnicraft range — the flowing curves, the sculpted legs, the tactile surfaces — exists because these craftspeople's hands made them possible.

"Complex designers choose Ethnicraft as their production partner precisely because our hands can do what machines cannot."

For your customers, this story has real commercial weight. When a customer holds the sculpted leg of an Ethnicraft table and feels the precision of the curve, they are feeling the result of a master craftsman's decision — made in an Indonesian atelier, carried across the world to their home.

Chapter Two

Soul Meets Precision

The most beautiful forms in furniture come from the marriage of human sensibility and technological precision. Neither alone is enough.

The furniture industry has long been divided: on one side, handcraft with all its warmth and variation; on the other, industrial production with its repeatability and scale. Ethnicraft chose not to choose.

Precision joinery in the Ethnicraft production process — where structural integrity and beauty meet.

CNC (Computer Numerical Control) milling technology was adopted not to replace craftspeople, but to enable them. CNC allows the cutting of precise, repeatable curves that would take a hand-craftsman days to achieve — and allows those curves to be reproduced identically across production runs.

But CNC alone produces only geometry. The surfaces it creates are machined — not finished. This is where the handwork returns. Every CNC-milled component passes through multiple stages of manual sanding, inspection, and refinement before reaching the finishing room.

The result is furniture that has the precision of technology and the warmth of human touch. You can feel the difference. The edges are not sharp or mechanical. They are eased, considered, and intentional.

"CNC gives us the geometry. The craftsman's hands give it life."

This is the story that transforms a furniture sale into a design education. When customers understand that the flowing leg of a dining table was first designed on a computer, then carved by a machine, then sanded by human hands multiple times — the price conversation changes entirely.

Technology in the Ethnicraft workshop

CNC Milling — what it is and why it matters +
CNC milling uses computer-controlled cutting heads to carve precise shapes from solid wood. A 5-axis CNC machine can cut complex 3-dimensional forms — organic curves, undercuts, tapers — with millimetre precision. At Ethnicraft, CNC enables designs like the BOK chair's compound-curved legs, which would be physically impossible to produce consistently by hand alone. The key advantage: every piece is identical in geometry, which means assembly fits perfectly and quality is consistent across production runs.
Manual sanding stages — why so many? +
A typical Ethnicraft piece passes through 4–6 manual sanding stages: coarse grit (80) to remove machining marks → medium grit (120) to smooth the surface → fine grit (180) to prepare for finishing → ultra-fine (220+) to achieve a silky surface that holds oil or lacquer evenly. Each stage is done by hand because machines cannot feel the grain direction, sense soft spots in the wood, or adjust pressure for curved surfaces the way human hands can. This is not inefficiency — it is craftsmanship at scale.
Oil vs lacquer — helping customers choose +
Oil finishing penetrates the wood, feeding and protecting it from within. The result is a matte, natural look and feel — the wood grain is fully tactile. Oil-finished furniture is easy to repair (re-oil a scratch and it disappears). It requires periodic maintenance (once or twice a year) which some customers see as a ritual and others as work.

Lacquer finishing creates a harder surface film on top of the wood. More resistant to daily water and staining. Less maintenance. The trade-off: if damaged, repair is more complex. For families with young children or customers in humid climates, lacquer is often the practical choice. For those who love the feel and story of the wood, oil is the purist's choice.
Kiln drying — the invisible quality step +
Freshly sawn timber contains 25–50% moisture by weight. Before any production can begin, this must be reduced to 6–8% for indoor furniture (8–12% for outdoor). This is done in kilns — large controlled-humidity chambers that slowly remove moisture over weeks. Why slowly? Because rapid drying causes stress in the wood that leads to cracking, warping, and checking. Kiln drying is the reason Ethnicraft furniture does not crack in your customer's home. It is also invisible, expensive, and takes time — which is part of why fast furniture producers skip it. You should explain this clearly, because it is a real differentiator.

Chapter Three

The BOK Chair Story

One chair. A perfect case study in how Ethnicraft's capabilities set it apart. Know this story cold — it sells itself.

Case Study — Icon in Focus

Designing the impossible. Then making it 10,000 times.

The BOK chair began as a design challenge: create a solid wood dining chair with organic, compound-curved legs that flows naturally from seat to floor without a single straight line. On paper, easy. In solid wood, at scale, with consistent quality — extremely difficult.

The challenge: organic curves in multiple planes simultaneously. A compound curve cannot be cut on a standard machine. It requires multi-axis CNC tooling, precise fibre grain alignment (so the wood doesn't split at the curves), and then — critically — a craftsman who knows how to sand a curved surface without flattening the geometry.

The result is a chair that looks as though it was born that way — as though the wood grew into that shape. That is the goal of great furniture making. To remove the evidence of effort.

Material European White Oak
Technique 5-axis CNC + multiple hand sanding
Finishing Oil or lacquer options
Design challenge Compound curves in solid wood at scale
Why it's special Fluid form that competitors cannot replicate

Retailer tip: When showing the BOK chair, ask the customer to close their eyes and run their hand along the leg. Then ask them to imagine how that curve was created. The story that follows — CNC precision, five sanding passes, a craftsman's inspection — creates the emotional connection that justifies every euro of the price.

Chapter Four

Vertical Integration

Ethnicraft controls the entire process — from the moment timber arrives to the moment a piece leaves for your showroom. This is not industry standard. This is the exception.

Most furniture brands are product curators, not manufacturers. They design, then outsource production entirely — and quality control depends on whoever picks up the phone that day. Ethnicraft made a fundamentally different choice: to control everything.

4–6 manual sanding stages. Human hands where machines cannot match.

Final inspection by trained eyes. Every piece approved before shipping.

This vertical integration is the reason Ethnicraft can guarantee consistent quality. When the same craftspeople work with the same timber species, on the same machines, following the same process year after year — the knowledge compounds.

They know how teak behaves when it is 7% humid vs 10% humid. They know how oak's grain direction affects which way a curve should be cut to avoid tear-out. They know that mahogany's fine, interlocked grain requires a sharper blade angle than oak. This is expertise that cannot be bought off a shelf.

For your customers, vertical integration means a simple promise: what you see in the showroom is what arrives at your home, every time.
