01

UX

How people experience the site
  • 01.1P0

    The first thing every visitor sees is a form — not the website

    • Before a single product, image, or piece of content is visible, the website asks visitors to choose their country and language in a pop-up window
    • On a phone, this pop-up takes up the entire screen. The website effectively does not exist until the visitor fills it in
    • This happens on every page, every time — even when visitors arrive directly on a Belgium or US page where the country is already obvious from the web address
    • It's the digital equivalent of a shop assistant blocking the door and asking for your address before you're allowed to browse
    • First impressions matter. This one says friction, not luxury
    See it yourself — open this link

    What we should do

    Remove the pop-up for visitors who already arrive on a country-specific page. Since the web address already contains the country (e.g. /en-be/ for Belgium), the website already knows where the visitor is. The pop-up should only appear when someone lands on the root page with no country in the address. This is a straightforward configuration change — about 2 to 3 days of developer work.

    High impact Cost: €800–1,200 Fewer visitors leaving immediately
  • 01.2P2

    The website has three distinct audiences but the navigation and content don't reflect that

    • Ethnicraft serves three different types of visitors, each with completely different needs: end consumers who are researching and want to be directed to a nearby store; retailers who stock Ethnicraft products and need to order via the Partner Portal; and Contract & Trade professionals (interior architects, project developers) who order for client projects
    • The main navigation — three items, Indoor / Outdoor / Collaborations — serves none of these three journeys explicitly. A consumer looking for a store near them, a retailer looking to log in and order, and an architect looking for contract specifications all land on the same page with the same options
    • The Partner Portal is accessible via a small link in the top utility bar, and product pages do mention it for professionals — but as secondary text. The question is whether the journey from discovery to registration is clear enough for each type of visitor
    • Note: we can only observe the public-facing website. Whether the full trade journey (discovery → registration → ordering) is working well in practice requires input from actual dealers and architects, not just a website audit
    The Partner Portal — separate domain from the main site

    What we should do

    Define clear entry points in the navigation for each audience. Consumers need a clear path to "Find a store". Retailers and C&T professionals need a clear, prominent path to the Partner Portal and its registration page. This doesn't require rebuilding the website — it requires a navigation and landing page strategy that acknowledges the three audiences exist and guides each one forward. Start by asking your sales team: where do new trade partners get stuck or confused?

    Medium impact Cost: €4,000–8,000 Clearer journeys for all three audiences
  • 01.3P2

    The navigation megamenu is well-structured — but every category address still shows an internal code

    • The Indoor and Outdoor menus have proper dropdown megamenus covering Tables (5 types), Seating, Sofas, Storage, Office, Bedroom, Accessories, Outdoor Dining, Lounge, and more — this is genuinely well-organised
    • The confirmed issue is the web addresses: every category page still shows an internal database code instead of a readable word. The dining chairs page address contains cat_furniture_seating_chairs instead of simply seating/chairs
    • This affects every category across both Indoor and Outdoor — making addresses impossible to read aloud, awkward to type or share, and harder for Google to interpret correctly
    • Two discovery paths are also currently missing from the navigation: browsing by collection name (Bok, PI, Osso, Geometric) and filtering by material (oak, teak, walnut) — both natural ways for designers and consumers to explore the catalogue
    Dining tables — see the internal code in the address

    What we should do

    Replace internal category codes with clean, readable URL slugs. This is a SAP Commerce configuration change — each category gets a human-readable alias (e.g. /indoor/tables/dining-tables). Proper redirects from old to new addresses ensure nothing breaks for Google or for bookmarked links. As a separate addition, consider a "Shop by collection" and "Shop by material" entry point in the nav — both missing discovery paths that suit the Ethnicraft catalogue well.

    Medium impact Cost: €4,000–7,000 Cleaner addresses + better Google indexing
  • 01.4P3

    You cannot configure a product anywhere on the website

    • Ethnicraft sells furniture in multiple finishes, sizes, and upholstery options — but none of this can be selected on the website
    • A customer who wants to know if the N701 sofa comes in a specific fabric has to call a dealer, send an email, or simply give up
    • At the price point Ethnicraft operates in, customers expect to explore their options online before committing. Brands like Muuto, Vitra, and BoConcept all offer this
    • The result: many potential buyers leave without buying because they can't get the information they need without picking up the phone
    • This is the single biggest missed sales opportunity on the entire website
    Product page — no options to select

    What we should do

    Add an online product configurator. This is a tool that lets visitors choose finish, size, and fabric directly on the product page and see the result visually. The product variant data (finishes, fabrics, dimensions) already exists in our back-end system — it just needs to be exposed on the website. A visual configurator would make Ethnicraft significantly more competitive online and reduce pressure on dealers to answer basic product questions.

    High impact Cost: €70,000–110,000 Online conversion rate +15–30%
  • 01.5P2

    The search bar only works if you already know the exact product name

    • Try searching for "large wooden dining table" or "warm oak bench" — searches that any real customer might type — and the results are poor or irrelevant
    • The search only understands exact keywords. It cannot handle a description, a typo, or a synonym
    • This matters more than it might seem: visitors who use the search bar convert to buyers at two to three times the rate of visitors who just browse. A broken search is lost revenue
    • There is no autocomplete (suggestions as you type), no "did you mean" correction, and no understanding of what the visitor is actually looking for
    Search: "large warm oak dining table" — see the results

    What we should do

    Replace the current search with a smarter search tool. A tool called Algolia understands natural language, corrects typos, learns from what visitors click, and shows results instantly as you type. It connects directly to our product catalogue and can be configured by the marketing team without needing a developer. The investment pays back quickly because search users are the most likely buyers on any website.

    High impact Cost: €20,000–30,000 + €900/month Search-to-purchase rate +20–30%

Partner Portal

Verified from live screenshots
How it actually works: The ordering portal is the main website (ethnicraft.com) when logged in with a trade account — not a separate platform. When a partner is logged in, the website reveals trade pricing, discount tiers, lead times, cart, checkout, and order history. The service.ethnicraft.com site is only the FAQ and support ticket system. There is also a separate resource hub at pages.ethnicraft.nl (catalogues, novelties, swatches) which is a completely different website with a different design. The findings below are based on live screenshots from inside a logged-in session.
  • PP.1P1

    The discount tiers are only revealed in the cart — not while browsing products

    • Confirmed from the cart screenshot: a prominent "Buy more, save more" banner shows 25% off up to $10K, 30% off up to $20K, and 35% above $20K — but this only appears once the partner has already added items to the cart
    • During product browsing, a partner has no idea what discount applies to their order until they've already committed to selecting items
    • For a C&T professional planning a large project, knowing the discount tier upfront would likely influence how they build their order — and could encourage larger single orders to reach the next tier
    • Hiding this information until the cart stage is a missed commercial opportunity

    What we should do

    Show the personalised discount tier on product pages and in the header for logged-in partners. A small persistent indicator — "Your current discount: 25% — spend $X more for 30%" — gives partners a real incentive to add more to their order while browsing, not only after they've reached the cart. This is a Spartacus component addition and a meaningful commercial change.

    High impact Cost: €3,000–5,000 Larger average order values per partner session
  • PP.2P2

    Company name is limited to 30 characters at checkout — many real company names don't fit

    • Confirmed from two separate screenshots (checkout and address book): "Klant- of bedrijfsnaam (max. 30 tekens)" — company name is hard-limited to 30 characters
    • Many legitimate business names — interior design studios, architectural firms, project companies — exceed this limit: "Van der Berghe Interiors & Design" is already 37 characters
    • A partner forced to truncate their company name on invoices and delivery notes looks unprofessional and creates administrative confusion
    • This is a SAP Commerce field length constraint — it can be changed but requires a back-end configuration update

    What we should do

    Increase the company name field character limit. 60–80 characters would accommodate the vast majority of real company names. This requires a configuration change in SAP Commerce's address model — a small back-end task. While in there, also verify phone number and address field lengths are sufficient for international formats.

    Medium impact Cost: €500–1,000 Removes a real friction point for professional users
  • PP.3P2

    The country field is blank at checkout — even for a logged-in partner with a known account

    • Confirmed from the checkout screenshot: the country dropdown is empty when a logged-in partner begins checkout, despite the fact that their account already contains country information
    • Requiring a partner to re-enter their country on every order adds unnecessary friction to a process that should be as fast as possible for repeat buyers
    • The address book also appeared empty in the screenshots — suggesting that either saved addresses are not being pre-loaded, or the test account has none saved
    • For a B2B partner who orders regularly, not having their default shipping address pre-filled is a noticeable inefficiency

    What we should do

    Pre-fill checkout fields from the partner's account and saved addresses. If a partner has a default shipping address saved, the checkout should load it automatically. The country field should at minimum default to the country associated with their account. This is a Spartacus checkout component configuration — it may already be supported but not enabled.

    Medium impact Cost: €2,000–4,000 Faster repeat ordering for existing partners
  • PP.4P2

    Order history shows totals but no product detail — you can't see what was in an order at a glance

    • Confirmed from the order history screenshot: the list view shows order number, reference, date, status, order type, and total — but no product names, quantities, or SKUs
    • To see what was in an order, a partner must click into each order individually
    • For a trade professional managing multiple client projects and referencing past orders for re-orders or specifications, this creates unnecessary clicks
    • A simple product summary (e.g. "3 items — Bok chair × 4, PI table × 1") in the list view would make the order history immediately useful without opening each order

    What we should do

    Add a product summary to the order history list view. Show the first 2–3 items from each order directly in the list, with a "+X more" indicator for larger orders. Also add a quick "Re-order" button at the list level for repeat purchases. This is a Spartacus order history component update.

    Medium impact Cost: €3,000–5,000 Faster repeat orders + better project management for partners
  • PP.5P1

    The Novelties Hub lives on a completely different website — separate domain, completely different design

    • Confirmed from screenshot: the contract partner resource hub (catalogues, novelties, swatches) is at pages.ethnicraft.nl — a completely different domain from ethnicraft.com, and a different country domain (.nl vs .com)
    • The design is completely different too: dark green background, white text, a different visual language to the warm beige of the main website
    • A partner navigating from the main website to this hub experiences an abrupt visual and contextual shift — it feels like a different brand entirely
    • This hub is clearly maintained manually and separately, rather than being integrated into the main platform — creating additional content management overhead

    What we should do

    Integrate the Novelties Hub content into the main website under a dedicated trade section. This content — new catalogue PDFs, novelties, swatch downloads — should live at ethnicraft.com/trade or similar, within the same design system as the main site. In the short term, at minimum align the visual style of the hub with the main website's identity so the transition between them doesn't feel jarring.

    Medium impact Cost: €4,000–8,000 (integration) Consistent brand experience for trade partners
  • PP.6P2

    Changing or cancelling an order requires leaving the site and filing a support ticket

    • Confirmed from the support FAQ: "You cannot change or cancel an order yourself via the Portal. You will need to submit your request via our Service Portal."
    • A partner who placed an order and needs to adjust a quantity or cancel must go to service.ethnicraft.com and create a support ticket — they then wait for a Customer Service representative to respond
    • For professionals managing time-sensitive projects, this dependency on manual intervention adds risk and frustration
    Order change FAQ — see the manual process

    What we should do

    Add self-service order editing for orders that haven't yet been confirmed for production. Allow partners to adjust quantities or cancel lines directly in the order detail page, with clear status indicators showing the cutoff point. This is a meaningful SAP Commerce + Spartacus development task but significantly reduces CS workload and partner frustration.

    Medium impact Cost: €10,000–18,000 Reduced CS workload + better partner experience

Design

How the site looks and feels
  • 02.1P2

    Every page looks slightly different — nothing feels intentional

    • Buttons look different depending on which page you're on. Fonts change size unexpectedly. Spacing between elements varies from section to section
    • This happens because there is no shared style guide — no agreed rulebook for how things should look. Every time a page was built or updated, whoever did it made their own decisions
    • The result feels assembled over time rather than designed with intention — which it was
    • This inconsistency subtly communicates a lack of care, which is the opposite of what a premium furniture brand should convey

    What we should do

    Create a visual rulebook — one set of rules that governs how everything on the website looks. This covers colours, fonts, button styles, spacing, and how every reusable element is built. Once this exists, every new page or update automatically looks consistent, and the team no longer has to make visual decisions from scratch each time. This work also feeds directly into the logo rollout and the mobile redesign.

    High impact Cost: €20,000–35,000 Brand consistency + faster future builds
  • 02.2P2

    The website could be selling any furniture brand

    • The warmth, craftsmanship, and character that make Ethnicraft products desirable are not present in the digital experience
    • Remove the logo and the website is indistinguishable from dozens of other furniture websites
    • The typography is functional but has no personality. The colour use is generic. Product photography is small and uncurated on category pages
    • Customers who pay premium prices for premium furniture expect the online experience to feel premium too. Right now it doesn't

    What we should do

    Define what Ethnicraft looks and feels like digitally — and apply it. The new logo project (already underway) is the right moment to do this. Define how photography is used, what the brand's tone is online, what colours and fonts are used and how. Then apply this consistently across every page. Brand and design must move together — a new logo without a new digital identity is half the job.

    High impact Cost: included in design system work Brand perception + premium positioning
  • 02.3P1

    There are no clear "next steps" — visitors don't know what to do

    • A "call to action" is the button or link that tells you what to do next on a page — "view the full collection", "find a store near you", "add to cart"
    • On the current website, these are either missing, too small to notice, or placed so low on the page that most visitors never reach them
    • On the homepage, there is no single clear action above the visible area when you first arrive
    • Every page a visitor leaves without doing anything is a missed opportunity. Clear next-step buttons are one of the simplest ways to improve this

    What we should do

    Redesign every key page with one clear primary action defined upfront. Each page should have one obvious thing it wants the visitor to do next. These actions should be visually prominent — high contrast, well-placed, with clear wording. This is a design decision first, then a small development task. Can be done page by page starting with the highest-traffic pages: homepage, collection pages, product pages.

    Medium impact Cost: €3,000–5,000 Click-through and engagement +10–20%
  • 02.4P1

    When the new logo is ready, it must change everywhere at once

    • The current handwritten logo no longer fits the direction the brand is heading
    • The new typographic logo (being developed by Robin and Nancy) will need to be applied across every digital touchpoint at the same time — not just the website header
    • This includes: the small icon you see in the browser tab, the preview image when you share a link on social media, email headers, the Partner Portal, and any digital advertising
    • A logo that's changed on the website but still old in emails or social shares looks worse than leaving it unchanged — inconsistency damages credibility

    What we should do

    Before the new logo goes live, map out every digital location where it appears and plan the rollout as one coordinated action. Update the website header, the browser tab icon, the social sharing preview image, all email templates in HubSpot, and the Partner Portal header — all at the same time. Assign ownership for each touchpoint before launch day.

    Medium impact Cost: €2,000–3,500 Brand coherence at launch

Technical

How the site is built
  • 03.1P1

    Google visits our website and sees a blank page — we verified this

    • We tested exactly what Google sees when it visits ethnicraft.com. The answer: almost nothing. No product names, no descriptions, no categories — just a blank container waiting for code to run
    • This happens because the website is built in a way that requires a visitor's browser to do all the work of assembling the page after it loads. Google doesn't wait for this to happen — it reads the page as it arrives
    • The consequence is serious: we are effectively invisible in Google search results for any product or category search. "Solid oak dining table Belgium", "ethnicraft bench", "modern outdoor furniture" — all searches we should appear in, but cannot
    • This is a structural issue with how the website was built — it was never set up to be readable by Google
    • This is the number one technical priority on the entire list
    What Google actually sees — view page source

    What we should do

    Enable the website to send fully built pages to Google instead of empty ones. Our website platform (SAP Commerce with Spartacus) supports this — it's called Server-Side Rendering and it is a standard option that was never switched on. Enabling it means Google can read every product name, description, and category from the moment it visits. This is the highest-ROI technical investment available to us right now. Every other improvement to Google visibility is worthless until this is done.

    High impact Cost: €30,000–45,000 Google visibility +30–50% → est. +€500K–1.5M/yr
  • 03.2P1

    Google doesn't understand what our pages are about — so it can't display them well

    • Websites can add a hidden layer of information to each page that tells Google exactly what type of content it's looking at: "this is a product page for a solid oak stool", "this is a collection page for outdoor furniture", "this organisation is called Ethnicraft and is based in Boom, Belgium"
    • We confirmed from the live website that none of this hidden information exists — Google has to guess what our pages are about based on text it can barely read (see issue 03.1)
    • When this information IS present, search results display more richly: a proper breadcrumb trail ("Indoor > Seating > Osso Stool"), a brand knowledge panel, and better categorisation in image search — all making Ethnicraft more visible when interior architects and designers search for inspiration
    • Brands like Muuto, Vitra, and Hay all use this correctly — even as catalogue/B2B brands with no direct consumer checkout
    • There's no consumer checkout to protect — every improvement here helps both architects searching for product references AND dealers searching by product name

    What we should do

    Add structured information to every page type so Google can properly categorise and display our content. This covers: product pages (product name, material, collection), category pages (breadcrumb trail), and the organisation itself (brand name, headquarters, website). This should be done immediately after fixing the Google visibility issue (03.1) — there is no point adding it to a page Google still can't read. No prices or reviews needed — this is purely about helping Google understand our catalogue correctly.

    High impact Cost: €8,000–12,000 (after 03.1) Better search appearance for architects, designers, and dealers
  • 03.3P0

    The list of all our pages is locked — Google can't access it

    • Every website should maintain a list of all its pages (called a sitemap) and share it with Google. Google uses this to find and index all content — especially new product pages and collections added after launch
    • Our list exists, but we checked it and it returns an "Access Denied" error. Google cannot open it
    • This means Google has to discover our pages on its own, like navigating a building without a floor plan. New collections may take months to appear in search, or never be found at all
    • The fix is a single technical change — 15 minutes of work — that would restore Google's ability to find all our pages immediately
    Our sitemap — see the Access Denied error

    What we should do

    Fix the access permissions on the file that contains the list of our pages. This is a settings change in one of our systems — about 15 minutes of work. Once done, submit the sitemap address to Google Search Console (a free Google tool) so Google immediately knows where to find it. This is the fastest win on the entire list.

    High impact Cost: €200–400 All new content found by Google faster
  • 03.4P2

    The same page exists in too many versions — we're competing against ourselves on Google

    • Our website has different versions for different countries and languages — Belgium in English, Belgium in French, the US, the Netherlands, and so on. Each is a slightly different web address for essentially the same content
    • When Google sees multiple versions of the same page, it gets confused about which one is the "real" one and splits its attention between all of them — which means all of them rank lower than they should
    • This is a known problem with international websites and has a straightforward technical fix — you simply tell Google which version is the "main" one for each country
    US version of the tables page

    What we should do

    Add tags to every page that tell Google exactly which version belongs to which country and language. This is a technical addition to the website code — it's standard practice for international websites and well within the capabilities of our current platform. The result is that each regional version of a page gets its full Google ranking credit instead of sharing it.

    High impact Cost: €6,000–9,000 International Google rankings improve

Speed

How fast the site loads
  • 04.1P0

    The website starts from zero for every single visitor — nothing is remembered between visits

    • We checked the technical settings and confirmed: the website is configured to never store or reuse any page. Every visitor, every time, triggers a complete rebuild of the page from scratch on our server
    • Think of a bakery that bakes one fresh loaf per customer on demand instead of baking a batch and serving from it. It's slower, it's more work, and there's no good reason for it
    • Caching — the technical term for storing and reusing pages — is one of the simplest and most effective ways to make a website faster. We are not using it at all
    • Google also uses website speed as a ranking factor. A slow website appears lower in search results, independent of everything else

    What we should do

    Switch on page caching and add a content delivery network. Caching means pages are built once and stored, then served quickly to subsequent visitors. A content delivery network means those stored pages are served from a server close to the visitor — a Belgium visitor gets the page from Belgium, a US visitor from the US, instead of everyone waiting for a response from the same server. We already use this for images — we need to extend it to the full website.

    High impact Cost: €3,000–5,000 + €1,500/yr Page load time –40–60% → sales +5–8%
  • 04.2P1

    Product photos are sent at full size to every device — phones get the same file as big monitors

    • A product photo taken in a professional studio can easily be 5–8 megabytes in size. A visitor on a mobile phone receives that same enormous file as someone viewing on a large desktop screen
    • This makes the website slow, particularly on phones — and for a brand where photography is the primary way to sell products, slow-loading images are both a performance problem and a quality problem
    • Modern web technology can automatically send a smaller, compressed version of each image to phones, and a larger version to big screens — with no visible quality difference
    • Our product images are stored and delivered through a system that already has this capability built in. It just isn't switched on

    What we should do

    Enable automatic image optimisation for all product photography. This means every image is automatically compressed for faster loading, converted to a modern efficient format, and resized to match the screen it's being viewed on. The system that delivers our images (Amazon CloudFront) already supports this — it requires configuration, not new tools.

    High impact Cost: €6,000–9,000 Mobile load time improves significantly
  • 04.3P0

    All text on the website is invisible for a second or two while the page loads

    • We confirmed this from the live website code: all fonts are set to hide text completely until the brand font has finished downloading
    • On a slower internet connection — which is still common on mobile — visitors briefly see a completely empty page before any text appears. This feels broken
    • The simple fix is to show text immediately in a standard system font while the brand font downloads in the background — visitors barely notice the switch, and the page feels responsive from the first moment
    • This is a one-line code change

    What we should do

    Change one setting in the website code so text appears immediately instead of waiting for the brand font. The text will briefly show in a standard font (like Arial) and then smoothly switch to Post Grotesk once it loads — most visitors will never notice. This is roughly 30 minutes of developer work and immediately improves the feeling of page load speed for all visitors.

    High impact Cost: €300–500 Better first impression on every visit
  • 04.4P0

    One of our marketing tools is freezing the entire website while it loads

    • We found this directly in the website's code: one of our HubSpot scripts is set up in a way that forces the entire website to pause and wait for it to fully load before showing anything to visitors
    • If HubSpot's servers are having a slow moment, our entire website freezes waiting for them — something we have no control over
    • Adding a single word to the code — "async" — would tell the website to load our content and the HubSpot script at the same time instead of one after the other
    • This is a 5-minute fix with no functional impact on anything HubSpot does

    What we should do

    Change how the HubSpot script is loaded so it no longer blocks the page. One word added to one line of code. While we're at it, move all marketing tools (analytics, ads, tracking) to a single management system (Google Tag Manager) so the marketing team can add, remove, and update these tools themselves without ever needing a developer.

    High impact Cost: €200–300 for the fix / €3,000–5,000 for full GTM setup Faster page load — 5-minute fix

Efficiency

How the team works with the site
  • 05.1P1

    The content editor tool exists but is limited in what it lets us do without a developer

    • We have SmartEdit — a tool that lets the marketing team update certain content on the website without asking IT. Banners, text, and some page components can be changed directly
    • The limitation is that only the components that have been specifically built and made editable in SmartEdit can be changed. Anything outside that set requires a developer and a deployment
    • In practice: the marketing team can update existing content but cannot create new page layouts, add new types of sections, or make structural changes. These all need IT
    • This creates a constant dependency between marketing and development — campaign timelines slow down, small updates pile up as IT tickets, and the team's ability to react quickly to opportunities is limited

    What we should do

    Expand what the content editor can do. Build a library of flexible, reusable building blocks — a large hero image, a text and image block, a product highlight grid, a promotional banner, an editorial section — and make all of these available to the marketing team in SmartEdit. Once this library exists, the team can build any campaign page on their own without writing a single line of code. This is a one-time investment that saves time on every future campaign.

    Medium impact Cost: €8,000–14,000 Marketing team self-sufficient — fewer IT tickets
  • 05.2P2

    Product information lives in too many places and frequently goes out of sync

    • The dimensions, finishes, availability, and pricing for each product should live in one place and flow automatically to the website. Instead, the same information is maintained in multiple separate systems — and they frequently fall out of step with each other
    • The result: the website shows products as available when they're not, shows missing finish options, or displays outdated specifications
    • For dealers and consumers making decisions based on website information, this creates mistrust. It also creates manual work for the team keeping everything updated

    What we should do

    Make our SAP back-end system the single source of truth for all product information. Everything — names, dimensions, finishes, pricing, availability — should be maintained there and only there. The website should pull directly from it automatically. Any external spreadsheets or separate systems currently feeding product data to the website should be audited and consolidated. This is partly a process change, partly a technical one.

    High impact Cost: €5,000–10,000 Accurate data → trust → conversion
  • 05.3P2

    Every decision about the website is a guess — we have no way to test what works

    • When the team changes a page headline, moves a button, or tries a different layout — there is currently no way to know whether that change helped or hurt
    • A/B testing is a technique where half your visitors see version A and the other half see version B, then you measure which one leads to more purchases or clicks. It turns design decisions from opinions into evidence
    • Without this, we're spending time and money on website changes that might be making things worse without anyone knowing

    What we should do

    Set up an A/B testing tool that the marketing team can use without technical help. Tools like AB Tasty allow the team to test different versions of a page through a visual editor — no coding required. Results are measured automatically against sales and engagement goals. Start with the most visited pages: homepage, collection pages, and product pages. Every test that produces a winning result compounds into permanently better performance.

    Medium impact Cost: €4,000 setup + €1,500–2,500/month Ongoing conversion improvement
  • 05.4P1

    We count visitors but can't answer what actually matters

    • Basic visitor numbers are probably tracked, but the meaningful questions go unanswered: which pages lead to purchases? Where do people give up in the checkout? Which collections get lots of views but no sales? Which campaigns bring in buyers versus browsers?
    • Without this, every commercial decision — what to promote, what to improve, where to invest — is based on intuition rather than evidence
    • The data to answer these questions is available. It just hasn't been set up to be collected and reported in a useful way

    What we should do

    Set up proper tracking for every meaningful action a visitor takes on the website. This means tracking: which product pages are viewed, when something is added to a cart, each step of the checkout process, completed purchases, use of the search bar, and use of the store locator. Present this in a live dashboard that the whole commercial team can access. Combined with the A/B testing tool (05.3), this gives us the feedback loop needed to continuously improve the website based on real behaviour.

    High impact Cost: €4,000–6,000 Data-driven decisions for all commercial teams
06

Under the hood

Technical findings from the live site
  • 06.1P0

    The internal address of our website's engine is visible to anyone who looks

    • We found this directly in the website's code: the private technical address of the system that runs our entire website is visible in every page's source — anyone can see it by right-clicking and selecting "View page source"
    • This is the equivalent of leaving the service entrance address and security code on the front window of a restaurant
    • It reveals which software provider we use, the name of our account, and the internal address of our commerce system — information that should not be public
    • The fix routes this address through our own domain so the underlying system is hidden behind ethnicraft.com

    What we should do

    Route the internal system address through a private Ethnicraft URL so the underlying infrastructure is never exposed. Instead of the full technical address being visible, all communication goes through api.ethnicraft.com — which we control. This hides the back-end details, adds a layer of security, and makes the system easier to maintain if anything ever changes on the back-end.

    Security Cost: €800–1,500 Reduces exposure of backend infrastructure
  • 06.2P1

    Visitors far from Europe wait longer — the website is only fast nearby

    • The main website is served from a server in Western Europe. A visitor in New York, Singapore, or Sydney has to wait for data to travel from Europe to their screen on every page load
    • Our product images already use a global network to serve them quickly everywhere — but the website pages themselves still come from one location
    • Extending the same fast global delivery to the full website (not just the images) would improve the experience for every international visitor
    • For a brand with a global audience, a US or Asian visitor should not have a noticeably slower experience than a Belgian one

    What we should do

    Extend the fast global delivery network we already use for images to also cover the main website pages. The infrastructure is already in place (Amazon CloudFront) — it just needs to be extended to the website itself. This is a configuration project, not a new purchase. Combined with the caching fix (04.1), this would deliver significantly faster load times for visitors outside Europe.

    High impact Cost: €2,000–4,000 + ongoing hosting costs Global load time –40–60%
  • 06.3P0

    The cookie notice may not meet EU legal requirements

    • We captured a screenshot of the live cookie consent notice and found that the Statistics category is switched ON by default when the banner appears
    • Under EU law (GDPR), non-essential cookies — including statistics and marketing tracking — cannot be pre-enabled. Visitors must actively choose to turn them on
    • As a Belgian company operating across the EU, this is a legal compliance issue. GDPR fines can reach 4% of annual global turnover
    • The fix is a settings change inside Cookiebot — our cookie management tool — not a website code change. It takes less than 30 minutes

    What we should do

    Log into the Cookiebot settings and switch Statistics and Marketing to off by default. Visitors will need to actively choose to enable them. Also verify that no analytics or advertising tools start running before a visitor has made their choice — this requires a quick audit of how our tracking tools are set up. Check the legal wording of the consent notice with our legal team to make sure it meets current requirements. Do this this week.

    Legal risk Cost: €500–1,000 Removes legal exposure — do this immediately
  • 06.4P0

    Visitors on mobile phones cannot zoom in on the website

    • We confirmed this in the website code: pinch-to-zoom is disabled on mobile devices — visitors physically cannot zoom in on any content
    • For anyone who struggles to read small text — which includes a significant portion of an older or visually-impaired audience — this makes the website difficult or impossible to use
    • This also violates EU accessibility law, which has been enforceable for private sector websites since mid-2025
    • Removing this restriction takes a developer about 5 minutes and has no negative side effects whatsoever

    What we should do

    Remove the line of code that prevents zooming on mobile. One setting, one change, 5 minutes. This should be done in the next code deployment regardless of any other priorities.

    Accessibility + Legal Cost: €100–200 Legal compliance — 5-minute fix
  • 06.5P1

    When something breaks on the website, no one on our team is told

    • Right now, if the checkout stops working on a particular phone model, if a product page crashes, or if a button stops responding — nobody at Ethnicraft receives an alert
    • We find out only when a customer complains — which means the problem may have been affecting sales for hours or days before anyone knows
    • A broken checkout that goes unnoticed for one working day on a website of this size is a meaningful amount of lost revenue
    • Error monitoring tools send an immediate notification the moment any error occurs, with full details about what went wrong and where

    What we should do

    Install an error monitoring tool that alerts the development team the moment anything breaks. A tool called Sentry connects to the website and sends a Slack or email notification — with details — the instant an error affects more than a handful of users. It is free for small volumes and takes about a day to set up. For any live e-commerce website, this is basic infrastructure.

    High impact Cost: €600–1,000 + €25–150/month Broken features caught immediately, not days later
  • 06.6P1

    Marketing tools have been added to the website one by one with no oversight

    • We found multiple tracking and marketing scripts embedded directly in the website code — including HubSpot running in two different ways, one of which is slowing down every page load
    • Over time, every tool added this way requires a developer to change or remove it, and nobody has a complete picture of what's currently running
    • On many websites, these accumulated tools account for a significant portion of page load time. They also risk collecting data before a visitor has given cookie consent — a legal concern

    What we should do

    Move all marketing and tracking tools into a single management system — Google Tag Manager. This is a free tool where all analytics, advertising, and tracking tools are added, managed, and removed through a simple interface — by the marketing team, without any developer involvement. It also connects to the Cookiebot consent system so tools only run after a visitor has agreed. Install it once, manage everything from it forever.

    Medium impact Cost: €3,000–5,000 Marketing independence + speed + legal safety
  • 06.7P2

    The website was built for desktop first — and it shows on phones

    • The website was designed with a large computer screen in mind and then adjusted to work on a phone. The result is a mobile experience that feels compressed rather than designed
    • On a phone today: the country selector takes over the full screen on arrival, navigation collapses without clear signposting, and some touch targets are too small to tap reliably
    • More than half of all website visits globally happen on a mobile phone. Google also uses the mobile version of a website as its primary version for search rankings
    • A website designed for phones first — and then adapted for desktop — produces a structurally better mobile experience

    What we should do

    When rebuilding the design system and visual identity, design for phones first. Start with the most important mobile journeys — arriving on the homepage, browsing a collection, viewing a product, completing a purchase — and design those for a 390px wide screen. Scale up to desktop from there. This approach is built into the recommended rebuild strategy (Section 08).

    High impact Cost: included in design system work Mobile conversion at par with desktop
  • 06.8P2

    The website is not usable for everyone

    • Around 15% of people have a disability that affects how they use websites — visual impairment, colour blindness, motor difficulties, or reliance on assistive tools like screen readers
    • Confirmed issues include: zoom disabled on mobile (see 06.4), which alone excludes many users with vision difficulties
    • Further likely issues (based on the type of website): product images without descriptive text for screen readers, low contrast between some text and background colours, forms that are difficult to navigate without a mouse
    • The EU Web Accessibility Act has required private sector websites to be accessible since mid-2025. This is no longer optional

    What we should do

    Commission an accessibility audit from a specialist. An auditor reviews the website against a standard checklist — covering screen reader compatibility, keyboard navigation, colour contrast, and form usability — and delivers a prioritised list of what to fix. Start with the high-severity findings. Budget for a second check after fixes are applied to confirm compliance. This protects both our users and our legal position.

    Legal + Inclusion Cost: €8,000–15,000 Legal compliance + broader audience

08

Rebuild or repair?

The honest verdict

Should we fix what we have, or start fresh?

Option A

Repair and improve the current website

  • The back-end platform (SAP) is already paid for and runs reliably — that's genuine value
  • The Partner Portal is solid and doesn't need replacing — it needs better visibility
  • The P0 and P1 fixes deliver real, measurable improvements within weeks
  • Less disruption — the team knows the current system
  • No migration risk or downtime
  • But: the website was not built to be read by Google. Fixing that on the current platform is expensive and complex
  • And: the developer tool used for the front-end (Spartacus/Angular) is difficult to hire for and slow to build with
Cost to reach a competitive standard: €150,000–250,000 over 12 months, plus SAP licence of approx. €80,000–160,000/year
Recommended

Option B

Keep the SAP back-end. Replace only the website front-end

  • The SAP back-end (the engine that manages products, pricing, orders, and the Partner Portal) is worth keeping — it works
  • Replace only what visitors see: the front-end website, built fresh on a modern technology (Next.js) that has Google visibility built in by default
  • Google visibility is solved on day one — no expensive retrofitting
  • The design system, configurator, and trade/consumer split are all far easier to build cleanly on a new front-end
  • Modern front-end technology has a much larger pool of developers — faster to build, cheaper to maintain
  • SAP itself officially recommends this approach for companies in our position
  • The SAP licence is retained — only the front-end changes
Cost: €200,000–320,000 for a new front-end over 12–18 months. SAP licence stays. Lower long-term maintenance cost.

Conclusion

Fix the critical issues this week. Plan a new front-end for 2027.

The website has three fundamental problems that together cost us significant revenue every day: Google cannot read our pages (so we barely exist in search), visitors hit a form before they see anything (so first impressions are bad), and the site is slow and uncached (so people leave before they've even browsed). These are not cosmetic issues — they are structural.

The right move is two tracks at once. Fix the P0 and P1 issues immediately — cost €40,000–75,000, real return within 3–6 months. At the same time, begin planning a new website front-end for a 2027 launch, built on modern technology that makes all the big fixes — Google visibility, speed, mobile, configurator — far easier and cheaper to achieve.

Do not spend €250,000 patching the current front-end. The structural problems are too deep and the technology too limiting. The SAP back-end is worth keeping. The website the world sees is not.

The total upside of doing this right: an estimated +€2–4.5 million in additional revenue per year. At a total investment of around €300,000, the return pays back within 9 to 18 months.

This is the starting point.
Not the destination.

Everything above is documented, evidenced, and costed. The P0 items can start this week — no strategic decision required. The bigger choices about the website's future can be made on the timeline set at the kick-off meeting.
